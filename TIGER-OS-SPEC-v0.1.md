# Tiger OS Specification v0.1

## 1. What Tiger OS Is

Tiger OS is an authenticity-first, continuity-preserving human–AI operating framework for reducing cognitive friction, protecting authorship, and enabling structured work across complex real-world domains.

It is not a generic productivity system, prompt pack, memory dump, or autonomous agent architecture.

Tiger OS is designed to support real-life work in conditions where meaning, context, authorship, and usability matter. Its purpose is not merely to generate outputs, but to preserve aligned function across interpretation, execution, adaptation, continuity, and review.

At a high level, Tiger OS exists to:

- reduce cognitive and operational friction
- preserve meaning across threads, tools, and contexts
- prevent premature canonization of local or fragile lessons
- support disciplined promotion of validated patterns
- keep human judgment primary at meaning-bearing boundaries
- enable structured human–AI collaboration without flattening authorship

Tiger OS therefore treats continuity as an architectural problem rather than a memory problem. What must persist is not just content, but also meaning, provenance, scope, standing, fit, and approval boundaries.

Tiger OS also distinguishes between the operating framework itself and the tools that may help build or support it. The framework defines principles, layers, rules, protocols, and boundary conditions. Supporting tools may later help with migration, routing, extraction, seed-pack generation, and continuity support, but those tools are not the system itself.

In this sense, Tiger OS is best understood as a structured operating model for high-fidelity human–AI collaboration under real-world conditions.

## 2. Core Principles

The following are the current high-confidence governing principles of Tiger OS.

### Structure reduces activation
Clear structure lowers cognitive and emotional load and improves function under ambiguity, activation, and complexity.

### Stability through clarity
Stability is achieved not by suppressing complexity, but by making the state legible enough to navigate.

### Meaning before expression
Interpretation, judgment, and actual meaning should be stabilized before communication shaping, adaptation, or audience-facing expression.

### Usability is part of correctness
An output can be semantically correct and still fail if it is not usable in the form required for action.

### Interface rules are structural
Formatting, rendering behavior, output contracts, and container-specific constraints are not cosmetic. They are part of system reliability.

### Specialization improves quality
Tiger OS performs better when threads and tools have bounded roles rather than trying to optimize all dimensions at once.

### Promotion must be disciplined
Useful material should not automatically become canon. Lessons must earn broader authority through validation, reuse, and bounded promotion.

### Continuity is architectural, not incidental
Carry-forward, migration, reseeding, provenance preservation, and transfer fit are core system concerns, not secondary conveniences.

### Migration is maintenance, not failure
Long-lived threads may degrade as containers even while their internal protocols improve. Reseeding and migration are therefore normal lifecycle functions.

### Human-owned judgment / AI-supported workflow
The human owns meaning-bearing judgment, direction, approval, and canon authority. AI supports structure, translation, calibration, extraction, and bounded production.

### Evidence outranks elegance
Live use, screenshots, failure analysis, correction loops, and real-world validation carry more weight than purely elegant abstraction.

## 3. Core Threat Model: Flattening and Drift

Tiger OS is built in response to a recurring systems danger: good structure can still fail when unlike things are flattened into false equivalence or generic residue.

The primary threat model of Tiger OS is **flattening and drift**.

### Flattening
Flattening occurs when distinctions that matter are compressed away in the name of neatness, portability, optimization, or summarization.

Tiger OS is especially concerned with flattening of:

- meaning
- authorship
- provenance
- scope
- standing or authority
- thread role
- mechanism
- fit to container or task

Flattening can make a system appear cleaner while actually making it less true, less usable, less safe, or less aligned.

### Drift
Drift occurs when material changes status, scope, wording, or authority without deliberate review.

Tiger OS is especially concerned with drift caused by:

- migration without preserved standing
- extraction that compresses away mechanism
- promotion without sufficient validation
- optimization that strips authorship or signal
- paraphrase that mutates meaning-bearing wording
- weak distinction between local lessons and governing rules

### Common failure forms

#### Flattened continuity
Material carries forward as generic summary residue rather than preserving meaning, mechanism, authorship, and fit.

#### Provenance drift
Material loses where it came from, causing external input, local notes, summaries, and canon to behave as if they have equal authority.

#### Scope drift
Thread-local, clinic-specific, or context-fragile lessons begin behaving like system-wide truths.

#### Validation drift
Candidate or provisional material begins behaving like validated architecture.

#### Authority flattening
Advisory signals, candidate lessons, and canon start behaving like the same kind of thing.

#### Agent-language drift
Language incorrectly implies that the thread or AI performed judgment the human actually performed.

#### Optimization flattening
Content becomes more polished or container-fit while losing mechanism, authorship, or truth-value.

#### Thread-role conflation
Reasoning, execution, synthesis, and communication shaping happen in the same place and degrade each other.

#### Status blindness
The system retains content but not the standing of the content.

### Why this threat model matters
Tiger OS uses layers, promotion buffers, calibration, validation, migration logic, and approval boundaries because flattening and drift are not edge cases. They are recurring failure modes in any continuity-seeking human–AI system.

The goal is not maximal compression or maximal memory. The goal is preserved function with preserved meaning.

## 4. Architectural Layers

Tiger OS uses layered architecture to preserve role clarity, reduce drift, and prevent unlike materials from collapsing into one undifferentiated system.

Some layers are current high-confidence canon. Others are strong emerging architecture that should be named explicitly without overstating maturity.

### 4.1 Canonical Authority Layer
**Status:** Current canon

**Purpose:**  
Holds locked rules, foundational operating models, hard constraints, standard operating logic, and architecture that has earned durable authority.

**Belongs here:**
- foundational principles
- locked wording
- stable rules
- validated architecture
- durable system constraints
- canonical reference material

**Does not belong here:**
- provisional lessons
- raw examples
- exploratory thinking
- loosely validated ideas
- early implementation experiments

This layer exists to preserve authority, prevent casual drift, and maintain a stable reference point for the rest of the system.

### 4.2 Synthesis / Promotion Layer
**Status:** Current canon

**Purpose:**  
Receives distilled outputs from working threads, classifies standing, buffers material before canon, and maintains promotion discipline.

**Belongs here:**
- distilled reusable patterns
- validated lessons under review
- promotion candidates
- structured extractions
- reusable principles not yet fully canonized

**Does not belong here:**
- raw archive material
- live exploratory processing
- generic memory dumping
- unfiltered conversational residue

This layer prevents direct jumps from local success to canon. It is a governed synthesis and promotion buffer, not a storage dump.

### 4.3 Specialist Execution Layer
**Status:** Current canon

**Purpose:**  
Provides domain-bounded working spaces where actual scoped work happens.

**Belongs here:**
- domain-specific reasoning
- operational workflows
- drafting
- applied problem-solving
- protocol-bound execution
- tightly scoped production tasks

**Does not belong here:**
- broad canon governance
- unsorted archival accumulation
- mixed-function system-wide processing
- premature promotion logic unless explicitly in scope

This layer exists because specialization improves quality. Tiger OS assumes that bounded function generally outperforms broad undifferentiated thread behavior.

### 4.4 Human Approval Layer
**Status:** Current canon

**Purpose:**  
Preserves authorship, validates meaning-bearing changes, approves canon shifts, and constrains automation at high-risk boundaries.

**Belongs here:**
- approval of meaning-bearing wording
- canon promotion decisions
- authorship-sensitive review
- direction-setting
- scope governance
- final judgment at protected boundaries

**Does not belong here:**
- silent automated rewriting of protected material
- automatic canonization
- unreviewed transformation of identity-bearing language

This layer is not merely a user preference. It is a structural requirement of Tiger OS.

### 4.5 Calibration Layer
**Status:** Strong emerging architecture

**Purpose:**  
Preserves mechanism, authorship, signal, and meaning-bearing structure when material is being adapted across containers or optimization regimes.

**Belongs here:**
- narrow calibration passes
- authorship-preserving review
- signal restoration
- agent-language correction
- mechanism-preserving adaptation
- container-fit without flattening

**Does not belong here:**
- broad uncontrolled rewriting
- generic polishing detached from meaning
- silent optimization that changes authorship or mechanism

This layer is strongly evidenced, especially in writing and communication contexts, but is not yet fully generalized across all Tiger OS domains.

### 4.6 Migration / Continuity Support Layer
**Status:** Strong emerging architecture

**Purpose:**  
Supports reseeding, structured transfer, continuity across thread boundaries, and migration without dragging degraded container state forward.

**Belongs here:**
- migration support
- reseeding logic
- transfer discipline
- continuity-aware handoff
- selective retrieval
- promotion-aware carry-forward

**Does not belong here:**
- naive full-context carryover
- transfer without fit or provenance
- continuity treated as raw memory loading

This layer is increasingly necessary, but its final embodiment is still unresolved. It may later take the form of a thread family, process family, tooling layer, or a combination of these.

### 4.7 Intake / Absorption Layer
**Status:** Emerging architecture

**Purpose:**  
Holds grouped raw or semi-processed material before promotion or synthesis so higher layers are not overloaded with early-stage or weakly filtered inputs.

**Belongs here:**
- grouped raw intake
- local but relevant material
- semi-processed notes
- external input awaiting evaluation
- early-stage collections not ready for synthesis

**Does not belong here:**
- canon
- heavily filtered promotion outputs
- role-specific execution that already has a better home

This layer appears increasingly necessary, but its exact form is still unresolved.

### 4.8 Interaction Lab / Evidence Layer
**Status:** Supporting component

**Purpose:**  
Stores concrete examples, screenshots, iterations, initial states, final outputs, and contextual nuance that should not be prematurely abstracted.

**Belongs here:**
- example-rich evidence
- screenshots
- before/after comparisons
- contextual nuance
- anchor examples for future interpretation

**Does not belong here:**
- premature abstraction into system-wide principles
- canon material unless separately promoted

This layer helps preserve evidence so future abstraction remains grounded rather than generic.

### 4.9 Control Room / Execution-Routing Layer
**Status:** Supporting component

**Purpose:**  
Acts as an active execution interface that selects relevant structures, frames decisions, and routes action.

**Belongs here:**
- routing support
- execution framing
- loading only relevant structures
- active coordination across bounded work

**Does not belong here:**
- full continuity embodiment by default
- uncontrolled global orchestration
- automatic authority reassignment

The final boundary between Control Room functions and a future continuity-support layer remains unresolved.

### Layer relationships
At minimum, Tiger OS currently relies on a stable separation between:

- canonical authority
- synthesis and promotion
- specialist execution
- human approval

Around that core, a broader support architecture is emerging:

- calibration
- migration / continuity support
- intake / absorption
- evidence anchoring
- execution routing

Tiger OS uses this layered model because continuity, quality, authorship, and usability all degrade when all system functions are forced into one undifferentiated space.

## 5. Thread Types and Governance

Tiger OS uses different thread types because different forms of work degrade when forced into the same container.

The governing assumption is simple: a thread’s **role** matters as much as its **topic**.

A thread about veterinary work, for example, may still need to be separated into distinct spaces for reasoning, client-facing communication, protocol extraction, or operational execution. Subject alone is not enough to determine fit.

### 5.1 One thread, one main role
Each thread should have one primary role.

This does not mean every thread must be absurdly narrow. It means the thread should optimize for one dominant function rather than trying to simultaneously perform:
- active reasoning
- communication shaping
- canon extraction
- archival storage
- implementation planning
- emotional processing
- publication calibration

When too many of these functions share the same space, role confusion increases and output quality degrades.

### 5.2 Specialist threads over mixed-function threads
Tiger OS prefers specialist threads because specialization improves:
- output fit
- clarity of instruction
- calibration quality
- recoverability after error
- continuity across time

Mixed-function threads may still exist, but they should be treated as higher-risk environments for drift, conflation, and cognitive overload.

### 5.3 Canonical authority threads
Canonical authority threads exist to hold:
- foundational operating models
- locked wording
- hard rules
- stable reference material
- validated architecture

They are not exploration spaces and should not be casually updated through ordinary working dialogue.

### 5.4 Synthesis / promotion threads
Synthesis and promotion threads exist to:
- extract reusable patterns
- classify standing
- buffer candidate material
- refine architecture without hardening it too early

They are not generic memory stores, and they are not the place for live exploratory processing.

### 5.5 Specialist execution threads
Specialist execution threads exist for bounded work such as:
- structured reasoning
- drafting
- workflow execution
- operational formatting
- domain-specific problem solving
- tool-specific troubleshooting

They should be scoped tightly enough that the thread can become good at its job without being continually destabilized by unrelated functions.

### 5.6 Protocol-bound operational threads
Some threads are best treated as protocol-bound operational environments.

These are threads where:
- inputs are structured
- output form matters
- role drift is costly
- consistency is more important than open-ended exploration

Examples may include parsers, invoice builders, schedule tools, or other high-repeatability execution spaces.

### 5.7 Evidence-layer threads
Some threads or subspaces function primarily as evidence anchors.

Their job is to preserve:
- examples
- screenshots
- iterations
- initial state
- final state
- contextual nuance

This helps Tiger OS avoid abstracting too early and losing the evidence needed to support later system claims.

### 5.8 Reasoning vs expression separation
Tiger OS often benefits from separating:
- meaning stabilization
from
- audience-facing expression shaping

In practice, this may mean one thread is used to:
- think
- interpret
- decompose
- decide

while another is used to:
- phrase
- calibrate
- container-fit
- prepare publication or communication

This separation reduces friction and protects meaning from being distorted by audience optimization too early.

### 5.9 Governance rules
The following governance rules are current high-confidence operating rules:

- One thread, one main role.
- Do not process in synthesis/promotion space.
- Do not expand scope unless needed.
- Minimal calibration first; add only missing specificity.
- Preserve thread fit during migration.
- Route by role, not just by topic.
- Do not treat general memory accumulation as continuity.
- Prefer bounded high-fit spaces over broad ambiguous spaces.

### 5.10 Governance goal
The purpose of thread governance is not bureaucracy.

It is to reduce drift, preserve fit, lower cognitive load, and maintain enough structural distinction that continuity can function without flattening unlike work into one container.

## 6. Continuity Architecture

Tiger OS treats continuity as a protected transfer problem, not a memory-maximization problem.

What must persist across time is not merely content. It is the structure around content:
- meaning
- provenance
- scope
- standing
- fit
- approval boundaries
- relevant evidence
- validated lessons

### 6.1 Continuity without flattening
The central continuity principle of Tiger OS is:

**continuity should preserve meaning, authorship, provenance, scope, and fit — not just content.**

Continuity fails when material survives only as vague summary residue while losing the distinctions that made it usable or true.

### 6.2 Preservation of authorship
Continuity must preserve who owned:
- judgment
- interpretation
- approval
- philosophy-bearing wording
- identity-bearing wording
- meaning-bearing decisions

This is why agent-language calibration and approval boundaries are part of continuity architecture rather than mere style preferences.

### 6.3 Preservation of provenance
Material should carry where it came from and what kind of thing it is.

Tiger OS is especially concerned with preventing:
- external input from behaving like internal truth
- local notes from behaving like system rules
- provisional synthesis from behaving like canon
- migrated summaries from inheriting too much authority

### 6.4 Preservation of scope
Continuity must preserve where something applies.

A lesson that is true:
- in one clinic
- in one conversation mode
- in one workflow
- in one phase of a project
should not automatically become system-wide doctrine.

Scope preservation is essential to preventing local success from hardening into false generality.

### 6.5 Selective retrieval over full carryover
Tiger OS prefers selective retrieval over full-memory loading.

The goal of continuity is not to carry everything forward. The goal is to carry forward what is relevant without:
- overloading the destination
- importing degraded container state
- blurring the current task
- widening scope unnecessarily

### 6.6 Recursive promotion of validated lessons
Tiger OS learns upward through recursive promotion.

A working-thread outcome may become:
- a local lesson
- a reusable candidate
- a validated pattern in synthesis/promotion space
- eventually, canon

This recursive structure is one of the main reasons continuity must be layered rather than flat.

### 6.7 Migration is maintenance, not failure
Threads can degrade as containers even while their internal logic improves. 
Tiger OS therefore treats migration, reseeding, and thread refresh as normal continuity functions rather than signs that the work itself has failed.

This distinction matters because it allows the system to preserve working logic while discarding degraded or overloaded containers.

### 6.8 Evidence-anchored continuity
Abstraction without evidence becomes fragile.

Tiger OS therefore prefers continuity mechanisms that preserve:
- anchor examples
- screenshots
- before/after states
- timestamps
- concrete cases

alongside abstracted principles where needed.

This keeps later synthesis grounded.

### 6.9 Human approval checkpoints
Continuity cannot be fully autonomous without violating Tiger OS boundary rules.

At meaning-bearing boundaries, continuity must pause for:
- review
- approval
- re-anchoring
- scope check
- authorship check

This is not inefficiency. It is part of how the system avoids drift.

### 6.10 Emerging continuity mechanisms
Several continuity mechanisms are strongly suggested by the current architecture, though not all are fully canonized yet.

These include:
- reseeding
- thread-bridging support
- structured handoff chains
- seed packs
- promotion-status handling
- calibration checkpoints during transfer

These mechanisms are directionally important, but some remain emerging rather than fully formalized.

### 6.11 Deferred continuity mechanisms
The following appear important but are not yet mature enough to canonize in full detail:
- full status-envelope schema
- source-status vs finding-status tagging
- complete transfer-stability taxonomy
- exact continuity-tool embodiment

These should be treated as open architecture questions rather than settled system law.

## 7. Promotion Model

Tiger OS uses promotion discipline to prevent useful material from hardening into authority too early.

The central rule is:

**Do not confuse usefulness with canon.**

### 7.1 Promotion through buffer, not direct jump
Working-thread output should not jump directly into canonical authority.

Tiger OS instead uses a promotion buffer, where material can be:
- extracted
- distilled
- tested
- compared
- classified

before it is given broader authority.

This is the main role of the synthesis / promotion layer.

### 7.2 Candidate before canon
A strong success is not the same thing as a governing rule.

Tiger OS therefore treats many promising lessons as:
- candidates
- field-tested candidates
- validated reusable patterns

before they become canonical.

This allows the system to preserve good ideas without pretending they are more settled than they are.

### 7.3 Promote principles, not raw summaries
Tiger OS promotes:
- patterns
- rules
- structures
- decision frameworks
- protocols
- reusable language

It does not promote:
- conversational residue
- raw back-and-forth
- loosely compressed recap
- unresolved exploration
- evidence that has not yet been abstracted responsibly

### 7.4 Do not auto-promote local rules
Local success may be valuable without being broadly portable.

Tiger OS therefore protects against:
- clinic-specific rules becoming global
- thread-specific dynamics becoming system defaults
- context-fragile insights becoming architecture
- advisory material hardening into doctrine

### 7.5 Likely current promotion statuses
The exact vocabulary is still being normalized, but the clearest current status distinction set is:

- **Local / context-fragile**  
  Real and possibly useful, but not yet suitable for broader reuse without distortion risk.

- **Candidate**  
  Promising and possibly portable, but still under review or under-tested.

- **Validated reusable**  
  Strong enough to reuse beyond the original case, but not necessarily canonical.

- **Canonical**  
  Authoritative Tiger OS material that has earned durable governing status.

- **External input for evaluation**  
  Material worth considering, but not yet internally validated enough to behave like Tiger OS truth.

This status model is strong enough to guide current reasoning, even if final naming may still evolve.

### 7.6 Promotion criteria
A lesson is more likely to deserve upward movement when it is:
- distilled
- reusable
- validated in live use
- likely portable
- bounded enough to govern safely
- unlikely to create drift if reused

A lesson is less likely to deserve upward movement when it is:
- unresolved
- example-heavy but under-distilled
- too local
- too fragile
- insufficiently tested
- overly dependent on one person, one thread, or one context

### 7.7 Promotion and human review
Promotion is not a purely mechanical process.

Some material may be empirically strong but still require human review because what is at stake is:
- wording authority
- philosophical fit
- authorship
- ethics
- scope of governance

Tiger OS therefore treats promotion as both a validation problem and an approval problem.

### 7.8 Promotion goal
The purpose of the promotion model is not to slow down learning.

It is to make upward learning disciplined enough that the system becomes more reliable over time instead of more bloated, more ambiguous, or more self-deceptive.

## 8. Validation Model

Tiger OS uses validation to determine whether something has earned trust.

Validation is not limited to “did this sound good once.” It includes live function, portability, interface fit, resilience under variation, and meaning-preserving integrity.

### 8.1 One good output is not enough
A single apparent success is not sufficient evidence for reliability.

Tiger OS assumes that confidence increases when a pattern survives:
- repetition
- variation
- correction
- transfer
- live use under pressure

### 8.2 Live-use validation
Real-world use matters more than abstract plausibility.

Tiger OS gives special weight to:
- pilots
- screenshots
- user correction loops
- observed failures
- observed recoveries
- successful operation under actual task conditions

### 8.3 Reliability under variation
Something is more trustworthy when it works across changed conditions.

Variation may include:
- adjacent domains
- altered instructions
- different containers
- degraded conditions
- response correction and re-run
- stress conditions

This prevents Tiger OS from mistaking one narrow success for durable capability.

### 8.4 Portability testing
A lesson should not be treated as reusable just because it worked in one place.

Tiger OS repeatedly distinguishes:
- local success
from
- portable structure

Portability testing is therefore essential before broader promotion.

### 8.5 Output-contract validity
A result may be logically correct yet operationally wrong.

Tiger OS considers validation incomplete if:
- formatting breaks usability
- rendering destroys function
- container constraints are violated
- execution form is wrong even though semantic content is right

This is why interface rules are treated as structural.

### 8.6 Calibration success testing
When content is adapted across containers or optimization regimes, validation must include whether:
- mechanism survived
- authorship survived
- human-owned judgment remained visible
- signal was preserved
- flattening was prevented

This is especially important for writing, communication, and other meaning-bearing transformations.

### 8.7 No-loss comparison after major rebuilds
After significant transformation or reconstruction, Tiger OS prefers a no-loss check where possible.

This means comparing:
- before and after state
- missing elements
- unintended mutations
- degraded structure
- silent omission

This matters especially in archival, rebuild, and migration contexts.

### 8.8 Human approval as validation class
Some validations are empirical. Others are interpretive.

When the protected asset is:
- authorship
- philosophical fit
- meaning-bearing wording
- scope of authority
- canonical significance

human approval is part of validation, not an optional extra step.

### 8.9 Validation goal
Tiger OS validates not just for correctness, but for trustworthy reuse.

The question is not only:
“Did this work?”

It is also:
- Did it work under real conditions?
- Did it preserve what mattered?
- Is it safe to reuse?
- Is it safe to promote?
- Is it still true in the form that survived?

## 9. Protocol Families

Tiger OS uses protocols to convert principles and architectural distinctions into repeatable procedures.

A protocol is not the same thing as a principle, rule, tool, or layer.

- A **principle** is a governing truth.
- A **layer** is an architectural role or location.
- A **rule** is a constraint or decision condition.
- A **protocol** is a repeatable procedure.
- A **tool** is a concrete instrument or runnable aid.
- A **module** is a packaged component that may include principles, rules, and protocols together.

Tiger OS uses protocol families to organize repeatable work without flattening unlike procedures into one generic method.

### 9.1 Promotion protocols
Promotion protocols govern how material moves from local use toward broader authority.

They are concerned with:
- whether something should remain local
- whether something is candidate-stage
- whether something is validated reusable
- whether something is safe to promote toward canon
- whether something should remain external input for evaluation

Promotion protocols exist because usefulness alone is not enough to justify authority.

### 9.2 Migration / reseeding protocols
Migration and reseeding protocols govern how work survives thread degradation, thread refresh, and cross-thread movement.

They are concerned with:
- what to carry forward
- what to leave behind
- how to preserve fit
- how to avoid importing degraded container state
- how to preserve meaning without full carryover

These protocols are central to Tiger OS because migration is treated as maintenance rather than failure.

### 9.3 Calibration protocols
Calibration protocols govern meaning-preserving adaptation.

They are concerned with:
- preserving mechanism during adaptation
- preserving authorship
- preventing optimization flattening
- protecting lived-use meaning
- container-fit without signal loss
- correcting agent-language drift

Calibration protocols are strongly evidenced, especially in writing and communication contexts, and may expand into broader Tiger OS use over time.

### 9.4 Output-contract protocols
Output-contract protocols govern whether the shape of an output remains usable in its actual container.

They are concerned with:
- formatting rules
- rendering dependencies
- copy-block discipline
- interface constraints
- structural output requirements
- execution-form correctness

Tiger OS treats output-contract reliability as structural, not cosmetic.

### 9.5 Approval-boundary protocols
Approval-boundary protocols govern when transformation must pause for human review.

They are concerned with:
- meaning-bearing wording
- locked or approved language
- canon promotion
- philosophy-bearing or identity-bearing language
- scope-sensitive transformations
- ambiguous authority shifts

These protocols ensure that sensitive material is not silently transformed under the guise of optimization.

### 9.6 Routing protocols
Routing protocols govern where work should happen.

They are concerned with:
- which thread type should receive a task
- when reasoning should stop and communication shaping begin
- when promotion should occur
- when content should stay local
- when migration support is needed
- when calibration is required before reuse or publication

Routing protocols are clearly necessary, though their final formal grammar is still emerging.

### 9.7 Extraction protocols
Extraction protocols govern how reusable signal is distilled from larger bodies of work.

They are concerned with:
- separating pattern from recap
- controlled passes
- category separation
- high-signal filtering
- preserving wording distinctions when meaningful
- producing reusable structures rather than conversational residue

Extraction is one of the clearest current Tiger OS protocol families.

### 9.8 Applied protocol examples
The following are strong current examples of individual protocols or candidate protocols within the broader Tiger OS system:

- **Threaded Extraction — Paced Mode**  
  Multi-pass signal extraction with strict filtering, controlled pacing, and stop conditions.

- **Attorney Agreement Clarification Protocol**  
  Term-by-term decomposition and negotiation support for high-stakes agreement review.

- **Legal / High-Stakes Folder Rebuild Protocol**  
  Verified staged reconstruction of high-risk document systems.

- **Clear Boundary / Preserved Bond**  
  Boundary-setting pattern designed to preserve clarity while reducing unnecessary relational severance.

- **Authorship-Preserving Prompt Design**  
  Prompt architecture intended to preserve human mechanism, authorship, and signal during specialized optimization.

- **Restoration Protocol**  
  Recovery logic for UI/output-contract failure and output-shape restoration.

Some of these are stronger as protocol examples than as canon-level universal protocols. Tiger OS should preserve that distinction.

### 9.9 Protocol maturity
Not every protocol is equally settled.

Tiger OS distinguishes between:
- stable protocol families
- validated individual protocols
- candidate protocols
- local or domain-specific protocols
- protocols that are still examples rather than canonized reusable modules

This distinction is important because protocol vocabulary can otherwise create false certainty.

### 9.10 Protocol goal
The purpose of protocols in Tiger OS is not to mechanize everything.

It is to make repeatable work more reliable while preserving truth, fit, authorship, and boundedness.

## 10. Human–AI Boundary Rules

Tiger OS requires explicit human–AI boundary rules because meaning-bearing work cannot be safely governed by output quality alone.

The goal is not to minimize AI involvement. The goal is to keep roles clear enough that authorship, direction, judgment, and accountability remain intact.

### 10.1 Human owns canon promotion
No material becomes authoritative merely because it was useful once, elegantly phrased, or successfully generated by AI.

Canon promotion remains human-governed.

### 10.2 Human owns meaning-bearing wording approval
Identity-bearing, philosophy-bearing, ethics-bearing, mechanism-bearing, or otherwise high-risk wording requires human approval before modification, promotion, or reuse as governing language.

### 10.3 Human remains scope governor
The human decides what a thread is for, what a system is for, what should remain local, and what is safe to promote upward.

AI may assist with suggestions, but not replace this authority.

### 10.4 AI may optimize within a bounded role
AI is strongest when the task boundary is clear.

Tiger OS allows AI to support:
- specialist production
- extraction
- draft generation
- structured interpretation
- bounded adaptation
- calibration support
- workflow shaping

Tiger OS does not assume that AI should simultaneously govern architecture, authorship, continuity, and promotion without explicit human review.

### 10.5 AI should not silently flatten mechanism
If causal structure, interpretive structure, or lived-use logic is the point, AI should not paraphrase it into generic framework prose just because that sounds cleaner.

Mechanism must survive adaptation when it is meaning-bearing.

### 10.6 AI should not overstate its own agency
Language should not imply that the thread or AI performed judgment the human actually performed.

Agent-language calibration is therefore a structural requirement, not a stylistic preference.

### 10.7 Protected wording requires caution
Locked wording, approved wording, or meaning-bearing wording should not be altered casually.

When wording carries identity, philosophy, ethics, authorship, or canonical significance, transformation should pause for review rather than proceed silently.

### 10.8 Human approval is part of validation
Some validations are empirical. Others are interpretive.

## 11. Failure Modes and Safeguards

Tiger OS assumes that failure will occur unless the system actively protects against flattening, drift, overreach, and role confusion.

For that reason, failure analysis is not auxiliary. It is part of the architecture.

### 11.1 Continuity failures

#### Flattened continuity
Material transfers as generic summary residue instead of preserving meaning, mechanism, authorship, and fit.

**Safeguards:**
- layered continuity architecture
- selective retrieval
- evidence anchoring
- signal-preserving calibration
- provenance-aware migration

#### Provenance drift
Material loses where it came from, causing unlike things to behave as if they have equal authority.

**Safeguards:**
- preserve provenance during transfer
- separate local, candidate, external, and canonical material
- require review at high-risk boundaries

#### Scope drift
Thread-local or context-specific lessons begin behaving like system-wide truths.

**Safeguards:**
- preserve scope
- distinguish local from portable
- require repeated confirmation before generalization

#### Validation drift
Provisional material behaves like validated architecture.

**Safeguards:**
- candidate before canon
- promotion through buffer
- validation under variation
- live-use testing

#### Migration drift
Context-fragile material is carried forward as if stable and portable.

**Safeguards:**
- do not auto-migrate fragile material
- preserve fit during transfer
- use reseeding rather than dump carryover

#### Context overload masquerading as continuity
Too much prior material is loaded, degrading fit and increasing drift.

**Safeguards:**
- selective retrieval
- route by relevance
- avoid full-memory carryover by default

### 11.2 Canonization failures

#### Premature canonization
A promising lesson becomes governing architecture before scope, limits, and failure modes are known.

**Safeguards:**
- candidate before canon
- require cross-context evidence
- treat single wins as insufficient

#### Canon bloat
Too much material enters canon, weakening its authority.

**Safeguards:**
- keep canon curated
- use synthesis/promotion buffer
- promote principles, not raw residue

#### Casual drift in canon
Core wording or governing logic changes without deliberate review.

**Safeguards:**
- locked wording
- versioning
- approval at meaning-bearing boundaries

#### Authority flattening
Candidate, advisory, local, and canonical material begin behaving as the same kind of thing.

**Safeguards:**
- preserve standing distinctions
- keep promotion statuses visible
- resist silent authority reassignment

### 11.3 Extraction failures

#### Category bleed
Architecture, execution, language, and failure logic get blended together.

**Safeguards:**
- controlled passes
- strict filtering
- one layer at a time
- stop when the pass is complete

#### Over-inclusion bias
Too much low-signal material is extracted.

**Safeguards:**
- high-signal filtering
- discard redundancy
- favor reusable structure over recap

#### Over-compression distortion
Useful nuance is lost through excessive compression.

**Safeguards:**
- preserve nuance where meaning depends on it
- prefer structure over hyper-brief summary
- protect meaning-bearing wording distinctions

#### Summary residue instead of reusable pattern
Extraction produces recap rather than architecture.

**Safeguards:**
- extract patterns, not summaries
- promote frameworks, rules, workflows, and clarified language

### 11.4 Migration failures

#### Authorship flattening during migration
Transferred material sounds AI-owned or thread-owned rather than human-authored.

**Safeguards:**
- human-owned judgment / AI-supported workflow
- agent-language calibration
- approval checkpoints

#### Role flattening during migration
Destination thread loses its scoped role because imported material carries mixed functions.

**Safeguards:**
- one thread, one main role
- preserve thread fit
- use structured handoff rather than content dumping

#### Legacy contamination
A new thread inherits degraded assumptions or clutter from an old one.

**Safeguards:**
- migration as maintenance
- reseeding
- carry structure, not accumulated instability

#### Context stripping
Material is moved without the local conditions that made it true or useful.

**Safeguards:**
- preserve provenance
- preserve scope
- do not auto-migrate context-fragile material

### 11.5 AI-assistance drift failures

#### Agent-language drift
Language implies the AI or thread owned judgment the human actually owned.

**Safeguards:**
- agent-language calibration
- authorship-preserving review
- human approval at sensitive boundaries

#### Optimization flattening
Output becomes cleaner or more polished while losing mechanism, signal, or truth-value.

**Safeguards:**
- signal-preserving calibration
- narrow correction instead of broad rewrite
- preserve mechanism when it is load-bearing

#### Premature machinery
Too much framework is added before the real problem is isolated.

**Safeguards:**
- build only the next earned layer
- start minimal
- add specificity only after actual failure appears

#### False-fix acceptance
A local symptom is solved while a larger governing rule is broken.

**Safeguards:**
- treat constraints as bundles
- identify the real failure layer
- reject fixes that damage core workflow integrity

#### Style drift mistaken for system improvement
Output becomes more polished but less true, less usable, or less like the human.

**Safeguards:**
- preserve voice fit
- prioritize truth and usability over polish
- use narrow calibration instead of total rewrite

### 11.6 Early or over-broad promotion failures

#### Single-win inflation
One success is treated as enough evidence for broad rule promotion.

**Safeguards:**
- reliability under variation
- candidate status first
- live-use testing across contexts

#### Portability assumption
A good idea is promoted broadly because it seems portable, not because portability has been shown.

**Safeguards:**
- portability testing
- map scope and limits
- delay canonization until broader fit is known

#### Local-success overreach
A thread-specific success becomes system doctrine too quickly.

**Safeguards:**
- separate local, candidate, reusable, and canonical material
- protect against automatic upward movement

#### External-input over-adoption
Elegant outside ideas become Tiger OS truth before internal validation.

**Safeguards:**
- external input for evaluation lane
- internal validation before merge
- provenance preservation

### 11.7 Strongest safeguard set
The strongest currently established Tiger OS safeguards are:

- mode separation
- synthesis/promotion buffer
- candidate before canon
- one good output is not enough
- selective retrieval over full carryover
- human-owned judgment / AI-supported workflow
- signal-preserving calibration
- migration as maintenance
- controlled extraction passes
- preserve provenance and scope
- build only the next earned layer

### 11.8 Failure-analysis goal
Tiger OS studies failure not to become pessimistic, but to stay honest.

A system that ignores how it distorts meaning, authorship, scope, or standing will eventually produce impressive-looking output that cannot be trusted.

The purpose of safeguards is to keep continuity, promotion, and collaboration aligned with truth rather than merely appearance.

- moving outputs between threads

- deciding what the next step should be
- maintaining role clarity during handoff
- preserving continuity without overloading the destination
- recursively stabilizing structure across multiple prompts

This rule protects Tiger OS from building automation faster than its validation and authorship safeguards can support.

This is a design constraint on current architecture, not just a long-term wish.

## 14. Versioning and Canon Governance

Tiger OS requires explicit canon governance because a system designed for continuity can still drift if its authority structures are weak.

The purpose of canon governance is to preserve stability without blocking legitimate refinement.

### 14.1 Canon must remain curated
Canon should remain:
- selective
- legible
- bounded
- authoritative
- resistant to accumulation for its own sake

Tiger OS does not treat canon as a full archive. It treats canon as a protected reference layer.

### 14.2 Canon must remain hierarchical
Not all material carries the same authority.

Tiger OS therefore distinguishes between:
- canon
- validated reusable material
- candidate material
- local or context-fragile material
- external input for evaluation

Even where exact status vocabulary remains under refinement, the hierarchy itself is non-optional.

### 14.3 Canon must remain versioned
Canon should not shift invisibly.

When canonical architecture, wording, or rules change, the change should be:
- reviewable
- documentable
- attributable
- explainable

Versioning protects against casual paraphrase drift and quiet mutation of governing logic.

### 14.4 Meaning-bearing wording requires special protection
Some lines are not merely convenient wording choices.

Where wording carries:
- identity
- philosophy
- ethics
- mechanism
- canonical significance

it should not be altered casually.

Changes to meaning-bearing wording require review before replacement, paraphrase, or promotion.

### 14.5 Promotion requires explicit review
Tiger OS does not support automatic canonization.

For material to gain broader authority, there should be:
- validation
- review
- standing distinction
- fit assessment
- scope assessment
- deliberate promotion

This is especially true when promotion affects governing architecture or protected wording.

### 14.6 Candidate material must stay visibly candidate
A candidate that looks canonical is a governance failure.

Tiger OS therefore benefits from:
- visible standing distinctions
- explicit labeling where possible
- separation between promising and authoritative material
- anti-flattening discipline in migration and extraction

Even before a full status model is finalized, this visibility rule is already structurally important.

### 14.7 Emerging architecture should not be presented as settled canon
Tiger OS intentionally includes architecture that is:
- current canon
- strong emerging architecture
- deferred or unresolved

This distinction should be preserved in documentation.

A system that hides its uncertainty will eventually confuse experimentation with law.

### 14.8 Future revision workflow
Future revisions to Tiger OS should aim to document:
- what changed
- why it changed
- what evidence justified the change
- whether the change affects principle, layer, rule, protocol, or implementation detail
- whether the change upgrades, deprecates, or narrows prior architecture

This helps Tiger OS evolve without losing continuity with itself.

### 14.9 Deprecation and replacement logic
Not all architecture should be preserved forever in active use.

Tiger OS should be able to:
- retire outdated structures
- replace narrower versions with clearer ones
- preserve historical reasoning without keeping obsolete rules active
- narrow overbroad earlier formulations where needed

Deprecation should be explicit rather than silent.

### 14.10 Canon governance goal
The goal of canon governance is not rigidity for its own sake.

It is to preserve enough authority structure that continuity remains trustworthy, promotion remains disciplined, and the system can evolve without dissolving into drift.
